Alexander Fleming

The Discoverer of penicillin

Alexander Fleming, the inventor of penicillin, died on March 11 in 1955. The Nobel Prize winning biologist is responsible for saving thousands of lives against fatal viral diseases.

    The road to penicillin, in no particular order:
  • Born on 6 August 1881 at Lochfield farm near Darvel, in Ayrshire, Scotland.
  • In 1903, the younger Alexander enrolled at St Mary's Hospital Medical School in Paddington;.
  • he qualified with an MBBS degree from the school with distinction in 1906.
  • During World War I, Fleming witnessed the death of many soldiers from sepsis resulting from infected wounds. Antiseptics, which were used at the time to treat infected wounds, often worsened the injuries.
  • In 1918 he returned to St Mary's. Alexander Fleming became a lecturer in Bacteriology.
  • In 1928 Alexander Fleming (1881–1955) discovered penicillin, made from the Penicillium notatum mold
  • Fleming’s legendary discovery of penicillin occurred in 1928, while he was investigating staphylococcus, a common type of bacteria that causes boils and can also cause disastrous infections in patients with weakened immune systems.
  • In 1946, Fleming succeeded Almroth Edward Wright as head of St. Mary's Inoculation Department, which was renamed the Wright-Fleming Institute.
  • Fleming was named rector of Edinburgh University from 1951 to 1954, freeman of many municipalities, and Honorary Chief Doy-gei-tau of the American Indian Kiowa tribe. He was also awarded honorary doctorate degrees from nearly 30 European and American universities.
  • Fleming died of a heart attack on March 11, 1955, at his home in London, England.
  • In 1943 Alexander Fleming was made a fellow of the Royal Society. In 1944 he was knighted.
